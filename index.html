1954 — 1968 & Beyond

The Civil Rights Movement

A chronicle of courage, sacrifice, and the long march toward justice in America

Scroll to explore the timeline

Scroll

The Civil Rights Movement was a decades-long struggle by African Americans and their allies to end institutionalized racial discrimination, disenfranchisement, and racial segregation in the United States. Through nonviolent protest, legal challenges, and extraordinary personal bravery, ordinary people changed the moral fabric of a nation.

1950s

The seeds of resistance — court victories, boycotts, and a movement finding its voice

May 17, 1954
Brown v. Board of Education
The Supreme Court unanimously ruled that racial segregation in public schools was unconstitutional, striking down "separate but equal." A legal revolution in three words: separate is unequal.
Supreme Court
August 28, 1955
Murder of Emmett Till
The brutal lynching of 14-year-old Emmett Till in Mississippi, and his mother's decision to hold an open-casket funeral, shocked the conscience of the nation and galvanized the movement.
Racial Violence
December 5, 1955
Montgomery Bus Boycott
Following Rosa Parks' arrest, 40,000 Black residents boycotted Montgomery's buses for 381 days. A young pastor named Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. emerged as their leader.
Nonviolent Protest
September 4, 1957
The Little Rock Nine
Nine Black students bravely attempted to integrate Little Rock Central High School. Governor Faubus called the National Guard to block them; President Eisenhower deployed federal troops to enforce their right to enter.
School Integration
September 9, 1957
Civil Rights Act of 1957
The first federal civil rights legislation since Reconstruction, establishing the Civil Rights Division of the Justice Department. Modest in scope but significant in principle — the federal government was back.
Federal Law
“

Injustice anywhere is a threat to justice everywhere.

— Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., Letter from Birmingham Jail, 1963

1960s

Direct action, sit-ins, and freedom rides — confronting hatred with disciplined courage

February 1, 1960
Greensboro Sit-Ins
Four Black college students sat at a whites-only Woolworth's lunch counter. When refused service, they stayed. Within weeks, hundreds joined. Within months, the tactic had spread to 54 cities.
Nonviolent Protest
April 15, 1960
SNCC Founded
The Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee was born out of the sit-in movement. Young activists — independent, bold, and uncompromising — would become the movement's most daring frontline force.
Organization
May 4, 1961
Freedom Rides
Interracial groups boarded buses into the Deep South to challenge segregated travel. Riders faced firebombings and mob violence in Alabama. Their courage forced federal desegregation of interstate transit facilities.
Direct Action
April 3, 1963
Birmingham Campaign
Children and adults faced Bull Connor's fire hoses and police dogs. Dr. King was jailed and penned his immortal "Letter from Birmingham Jail." The world watched, and could no longer look away.
Direct Action
June 12, 1963
Assassination of Medgar Evers
NAACP field secretary Medgar Evers was shot in his driveway by a white supremacist. His killer walked free for 31 years. Evers had given everything to the cause of voter registration and equality.
Assassination
August 28, 1963
March on Washington
250,000 people stood before the Lincoln Memorial. Dr. King declared: "I have a dream." It became the most iconic speech in American history and the moral heart of the movement.
Historic March
September 15, 1963
16th Street Baptist Church Bombing
A KKK bomb killed four young girls — Addie Mae Collins, Cynthia Wesley, Carole Robertson, and Carol Denise McNair — during Sunday services in Birmingham. The nation wept and demanded action.
Racial Terror
“

We shall overcome because the arc of the moral universe is long, but it bends toward justice.

— Dr. Martin Luther King Jr., 1965

1964–1965

Hard-won laws change the fabric of American democracy

July 2, 1964
Civil Rights Act of 1964
Signed by President Johnson after a 60-day Senate filibuster, this landmark law banned discrimination in employment and public life based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. A seismic legal transformation.
Federal Law
Summer 1964
Mississippi Freedom Summer
Hundreds of volunteers came to Mississippi to register voters. Three workers — Schwerner, Goodman, and Chaney — were murdered by the Klan. Their deaths shocked the nation into demanding the Voting Rights Act.
Voter Registration
March 7, 1965
Bloody Sunday — Selma
State troopers attacked 600 peaceful marchers with clubs and tear gas on the Edmund Pettus Bridge as television cameras rolled. John Lewis's skull was fractured. America watched. Congress acted.
Historic March
August 6, 1965
Voting Rights Act of 1965
The most powerful voting rights legislation in American history — banning literacy tests and all discriminatory voter barriers. Within three years, nearly one million Black Southerners were newly registered to vote.
Federal Law
“

Do not get lost in a sea of despair. Be hopeful, be optimistic. Our struggle is not the struggle of a day, a week, a month, or a year — it is the struggle of a lifetime.

— Rep. John Lewis

Late 1960s

Assassinations, uprisings, and the final milestones of a transformed nation

August 11, 1965
Watts Uprising
Five days after the Voting Rights Act was signed, six days of rebellion erupted in Los Angeles. Watts revealed that de facto segregation and poverty in Northern cities demanded a different kind of struggle entirely.
Urban Uprising
June 1966
Black Power Movement
Stokely Carmichael's call for "Black Power" marked a pivotal shift — from integration toward self-determination, racial pride, and community control. The Black Panther Party was founded in Oakland that October.
Movement Shift
June 12, 1967
Loving v. Virginia
The Supreme Court unanimously struck down all laws banning interracial marriage, affirming that "the freedom to marry" is a fundamental right. The case was brought by Mildred and Richard Loving, who simply wanted to live as a family in their home state.
Supreme Court
April 4, 1968
Assassination of Dr. King
Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. was shot on the balcony of the Lorraine Motel in Memphis. He was 39. Riots erupted in over 100 cities. The world lost its greatest voice for nonviolent justice. His dream endured.
Assassination
April 11, 1968
Fair Housing Act of 1968
Signed one week after Dr. King's assassination, the Fair Housing Act prohibited racial discrimination in the sale and rental of housing — the final landmark legislation of the civil rights era.
Federal Law
